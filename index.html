Kelly Criterion Project

Website


A Mathematics professor at Villanova reached out to me to create a website for him after a recommendation from Dr. Diaz-Lopez, the professor with whom I completed Mathematics research. Over the summer, we discussed over email that he needed a website that demonstrated the Kelly Criterion in the form of a betting game. The requirements he sent me were as follows:

  • A person opening the website would be given an account with starting capital of $100
  • They’d be able to choose a percentage of their current capital (from 0 to 100% inclusive, in whole percentages) to bet on heads for the next coin toss.
  • The coin toss would have a probability of p = 0.6 to come up heads. The probability p should be adjustable.
    • If the coin comes up heads and the player wins, the player would have the amount bet added to their current capital.
    • If the coin comes up tails and the player loses, the player’s capital would be reduced by the amount bet.
  • Repeat until a maximum of N=100 coin tosses is reached. This N also needs to be adjustable.
  • Each time, the player first chooses the percentage to play, then the coin “is tossed” (via a random number generator), and the win/loss added to the account.
  • If the player reaches a maximum of $1000 or above, at any time of the game, the game ends, and the player is given the $1000.
  • If the player ever bets 100% and loses, the game also ends for the player. The player’s payoff is $0.
  • If the player does not reach the maximum, the player receives the amount in their account after 100 games.
I worked on this project over the summer and emailed multiple versions of the website to him for comments. These comments were taken into account and I edited the website accordingly. The method for data collection I used took the results of each game and submitted them to a Google Form. I set up a Google Sheet to automatically format the received data. The professor was able to access, edit, and analyze the data. The link to this website is above. The professor is no longer using the website, so any data submitted to the website will still be sent to the Google Sheet, but it will not be analyzed.

The Language of Over-Consumption: Insights from YouTube Haul Transcripts

Undergraduate Research Assistant

I worked on this project with a Computer Science research team under Dr. Sarah Cooney, the same people from my #AntiHaul project. It references back to my research regarding #sheinhaul and #thrifthaul. It is categorized as SHCI, a field exploring Sustainable Human-Computer Interaction. Presented Poster

#Haul: An Analysis of YouTube Discourse Surrounding Over-Consumption and Sustainability in Haul Videos

MATCH Program
Published Paper

It focused on the #haul trend on YouTube, specifically examining #sheinhaul and #thrifthaul. It is categorized as SHCI, a field exploring Sustainable Human-Computer Interaction. Non-Recursive Solution of the Tower of Hanoi

Co-Master Program
Published Paper Website

I worked on this project with a Mathematics research team under Dr. Alexander Diaz-Lopez. It applied through Villanova's Co-Master Program and was chosen by Dr. Diaz-Lopez to join the team. We explored the Tower of Hanoi and its known, most efficient solution. We aimed to find an equation which could determine the peg that any given disk was on at any given move. As the only Computer Scientist on the team, I took on the role of creating simulations of the game to verify our equation on millions of inputs. I created these programs using Python and shared them with the team. We then proceeded to verify our solution through mathematical proofs. Our team created a slideshow and presented at the EPaDel conference at Albright College and Villanova's Mathematics Symposium. In addition, I created an informal website where users could input any move number and see the location of every disk in a 50-disk Tower of Hanoi. It was intended to be accepted on mobile devices by people attending the conference and is available at the above link. Additionally, we submitted our final paper to Mathematics Magazine where it was officially published in July 2025.
